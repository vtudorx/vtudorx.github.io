I've broken enough production systems to know that the hardest part isn't the code. It's the decisions that happen before anyone writes a line.

I'm a System Architect. My work sits at the point where business goals need to become technical decisions: what to build, how to run it, how to secure it, and how to keep delivery moving when the first answer was wrong.

My main stack is Go, PHP/Symfony, Python, and AWS CDK. On AWS, I work across EKS, Lambda, ECS/Fargate, Aurora, DynamoDB, EventBridge, S3, Transit Gateway, IAM, and Cognito.

Recently I've been building AI-enabled business intelligence using AWS Bedrock, Claude, Tableau, and custom MCP sidecars. The goal is simple: let stakeholders ask business questions in plain language and get useful answers from data they already own. No dashboard sprawl. No waiting three days for a report to run.

My background mixes hands-on engineering with technical leadership. I usually start by building a proof-of-concept around something unclear. But figuring out which ones are worth turning into something that runs reliably in production, without heroics, is most of the actual work. So that means making the domain model explicit, choosing module boundaries carefully, and keeping infrastructure costs visible. And documenting decisions through ADRs, shared standards, and engineering workshops so the next person doesn't have to reconstruct the reasoning from scratch when something breaks at 2am.
